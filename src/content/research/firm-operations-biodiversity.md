abstract: >-
  This study investigates whether biodiversity loss is internalized through
  market pricing or government intervention. Empirical results reveal that
  emissions from toxic facilities increase lead concentrations and decrease
  animal populations within a 12-kilometer radius. The US equity market
  ignores this biodiversity loss, as firms emitting pollutants near biodiverse
  regions attract no additional risk premium and face economically small
  penalties from regulatory action. A moral hazard issue likely drives these
  observations: firms located near ecologically sensitive areas underreport
  their environmental impacts both in financial disclosures and to regulatory
  oversight bodies.

This paper asks whether anyone — investors or regulators — disciplines the
biodiversity damage caused by firms' toxic emissions. Combining the EPA's
Toxics Release Inventory with ecological monitoring data, it documents real
ecological harm around polluting facilities, but finds little evidence that
equity markets or regulatory penalties price that harm. Firms near
ecologically sensitive areas also underreport their environmental impacts,
pointing to a moral hazard problem in environmental disclosure.
